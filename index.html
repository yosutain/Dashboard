There's a Certain Slant of Light - Analysis

page 1

This essay will go into the poem «There's a Certain Slant of Light» by Emily Dickinson. The structures of the poem will be looked at after the meanings of the poem become more clear, but the focal point for most of the following analysis is the lyrical content, and therefore only the structures that seem relevant for one's understanding of the text will be discussed. When one looks at this poem, like other poems by Emily Dickinson, one sees that the words therein are all chosen with such consideration that removing a single one would have drastic consequenses. Because of this, it is vital for one's comprehension of her work to look at these poems slowly. This analysis will therefore not rush through the poem, but devote a paragraph to every two lines of the poem, effectively splitting the poem into eight parts.

The two first lines brings a light into existence, a light that can only be seen on winter afternoons (ll. 1-2). This conjures up images of a cold winter where the light has found its way into the room of the persona writing the poem. Light is mostly seen as a positive word that represents life, enlightenment, ideas and other positive aspects of this world. This certain light, however, is slanting and not a light that would be seen as a prime example of something positive. If something is slanting one would in most cases want to fix whatever is slanting and make it straight or right again. These two lines would seem quite serene if one stopped reading at this point, but that which follows deals with the darker aspects of life.

Lines three and four give the light the attribute of being oppressing and heavy (l. 3). This gives a clue that the light might not be a light, but more a feeling that can be experienced on these winter afternoons. The heavy-laden feeling is compared with that of cathedral tunes, something that is never more oppressing than when one associates it with funerals and death (l. 4). Light is also not something that is commonly thought to have weight, and the choice of the word might therefore be symbolic. The meaning chosen in this analysis is that the light represents life, and that at certain times life feels dreadful and heavy.

The second stanza says that this feeling gives us «heavenly hurt» (l. 5). These two words are not usual to see together, as pain and hurting are rarely seen as something from the heavens. When one sees the light as life, then this divine despair would be something that

page 2

could only be caused by life, and therefore it would come from the heavens and be good in some way. One way of looking at this would be to see the winter afternoons as the periods of thanatophobia most people go through. Fear of death and the unknown, the uncertainty of what happens after life in this form, and that it will one day end. Such despair is in itself divine and would not exist where there is no life to be lost. The second line of the stanza further supports this idea as it says there is no scar to be found (l. 6). Not only does this signal that the pain is not what is referred to as physical «hurt», but rather something emotional, it also says that there is no emotional scar. A despair of life and death is one that becomes a heavy burden, but once the despair has vanished there is no scar left behind.

The third and fourth lines of the stanza say that there is internal difference that can be found and that this is where the meanings are (ll. 7-8). This internal difference might be the difference one can find in one's self after the despair has vanished. After longer or shorter struggles with the meaning of life and death, one often reaches a point of acceptance or even enlightenment. This is what one is left with instead of a scar, and this is in itself divine. «Heavenly hurt» is heavenly in that it leaves one with personal growth instead of emotional scars.

The first line of the third stanza reads «none may teach it - any -» (l. 9). What one learns from the divine despair is not something that can be taught, but one must experience it firsthand and struggle with the burden oneself to reap the rewards in form of personal growth. The separation of «any» makes it clear that one can not comprehend the slightest amount of such knowledge without introspection «on winter afternoons». The second line of the stanza is «seal despair» (l. 10). Ruling out that this is the despair of fin-footed mammals, this implies that the despair is something sealed within the person. A seal is also something that has significant importance and is official. This means that the despair is in itself important in the same way that life is said to have a value that can not be compared with the value of other things or explained in any way.

The third line of the third stanza says that it is «an imperial affliction», something relating to an empire (l. 11). The empire that this refers to is likely to be the human empire, that the «heavenly hurt» is a feeling belonging to all humankind. «Heavenly hurt» is the seal that makes one, officially, a part of the human empire. One could argue that this is a statement

page 3

saying that this feeling is something rooted in the human conscience to such an extent that it defines humans and defines life. Maybe these feelings and thoughts can be seen as an essencial part of «cogito ergo sum»; I experience divine despair and therefore I am not only alive, but also a part of the empire in which all humankind belong. The next line says that the imperial affliction is something that has been «sent us of the air»; from the heavens above (l. 12). This line makes it clear that the persona is not just referring to personal growth that will be achieved, but also enlightenment in the form of believing in a higher force of the kind people refer to as a God. The divine despair has been sent by the sole deity said to have created all humankind in his image. This «heavenly hurt» might even be caused by his being remaining obscured from the eyes of all humankind. God has planted the uncertainty and despair as an imperial seal, making it official that the life being lived is that of a human and not that of any other being that walks this earth.

The fourth and last stanza starts by saying that when the slanting of the light that is divine despair comes, the landscape listens (l. 13). This line is easier to understand now that God has been established as a part of the poem. God is the only being that could interfer with nature in a way that makes it listen. When life comes, and the seal of despair with it, nature listens to this and maybe even helps with the personal growth. The second line of the stanza says that shadows hold their breath (l. 14). Shadows will have to wait till the light is gone before they can have their turn. Light and darkness here would represent life and death. These states can not occur at the same time as one can not be alive and dead in the same moment. Breathing is also something that can symbolize life, and one can therefore see that there might also be life in death. This life could be what in Christianity is referred to as the afterlife.

The third and fourth lines of the last stanza read «when it goes, 'tis like the distance on the look of death» (ll. 15-16). When the light goes out, and there is only shadows left, the contrast between life and death becomes more clear. The distance between what is living and what is dead is uncomprehendable till the moment comes and the lights fade away. This might also be referring to the distant look one might see on many a dying man. Maybe this distant look is caused by the dying person seeing what comes as the lights turn dim.

If one were to sum up the contents of each stanza one might say that the first stanza introduces the weight of life, the second stanza says that the despair one feels in life is what

page 4

helps one grow and gain acceptance, the third stanza says that this despair is something received by god and is what everyone will have to face in order to gain knowledge that cannot be learned in any other way, and the last stanza is about how death is very different from life, but that there are positive aspects of death as well. The poem starts with life and ends with death.

There are a great deal of emotions in the poem, some which are reinforced by the use of alliteration. In the first stanza there is much use of s's like «there's», «certain», «slant», «afternoons», «oppresses», «tunes». This hissing sound is one that often reminds one of snakes, which in turn reminds one of venom and death, but most of all it signals impending danger and fear for what is to come. Such a signal is appropriate in the beginning of the poem as the poem grows darker as it goes. «Heavenly hurt» is another alliteration that gives the words more impact. One might be tempted to compare these h's with the grasping for breath that often comes with certain despair. The third and last alliteration that will be mentioned in this essay is the heavy d's. They are not found close to each other, but always in the end of a line. The words are «difference», «despair», «distance», and «death». All these words are heavy words that become even heavier because of their position in the poem, and because of the heavy consonants they begin with.

Another tool that is used in this poem, one that Emily Dickinson is particularily known for using, is fragments and dashes. The dashes have been used especially to create more focus on certain words and phrases. One finds this in the first line of the third stanza; «none may teach it - any -». Here the dashes contribute to single out «any» and emphasize that there is not anything of this that can be taught. In the second line of the fourth stanza the words «hold their breath» is bound by dashes and makes one look more at the process of holding their breath, than the shadows themselves. There are also similar device used to single out words that would normally get less attention without the separation. One example of this is «are» in the fourth line of the second stanza: «Where the Meanings, are -». The separation here has not been achieved by dashes, but by a comma in a peculiar position. These separating devices, as well as ungrammatical sentences, create the fragments that Emily Dickinson has become famous for using in her poems.

Emily Dickinson is an experimental poet who often takes a formal poetic structure as

page 5

the base of her poems, but then she makes small alterations that result in the poems being slightly different from the predefined structure. In «There's a Certain Slant of Light» she uses an ABCB rhyme scheme for the first stanza, but changes to the rhyme scheme ABAB in the last stanza. Here the change could represent the end of despair. Even though there is a certain regularity in the ABCB rhyme scheme, there are pieces that will not fit. In the same way as the persona in the poem is in despair and does not know how to piece his or her life together, the rhyme scheme is signalling that there is a struggle going on to find a meaning that can make his or her life come to order. In the last stanza there is an order in the persona's life that might not be found till death. When life has been resolved in the last stanza, there is order in the structure that signals the same.

In the work of Emily Dickinson one can also see that she is not a confessional poet, but creates various poetic personae that might be found behind the poems. The persona in «there's a certain slant of light» might be a person who's life is coming to an end. This persona tells us what he or she feels and describes the despair one has in life and how this will eventually come to an end. Only by creating a different persona could one talk about the entire life from a confessional viewpoint including the last moments when one rarely is capable of writing down experiences in such detail.

It seems appropriate to finish this essay with a few words about the poem as a whole. Emily Dickinson has written a very compact poem that encompasses the despairs of life and the transition one faces in relation to death. When it comes to the emotional part, it could be said that the poem is concerned with the darker aspects of life, but has a positive belief in that all is right and will lead to a better tomorrow. There is a ray of light shining through the darkness if you will. «There's a certain slant of light» takes up important issues of life in a very serious tone. One could analyse the meaning of the poem, like most poems, in many different ways, but the subject at hand will always be a serious one pertaining to life and death. Emily Dickinson is not a person whom people know a great deal about, and that gives one more freedom in ways of looking at the poem. However, from what one knows about her life, and by looking at her other works, there is good reason to believe that God plays a central role in the meaning of the poem. All in all it is a remarkable poem worthy of much consideration, as well as the fame it has gained throughout the years.

University does not prepare you for the working world.

It has been stated that universities do not prepare one for the working world one will face after the completion of one's studies. Whether or not this is true is dependant on a number of factors such as what one studies and where this studying is taking place. There is, however, a number of factors that are of a more general nature.

Most courses taught in universities today require a significant amount of independent study and thought. This increases one's ability of critical thinking, as well as teaching one the skills necessary to learn independently. These are skills that come in handy in practically every occupation. There is, however, no perfect substitute for real world experience. The workplace might have a different approach to problems, and then the years one has spent in a university might influence one to go against the accepted norms of said workplace. Although this might be true in some cases, students are subjected to a number of different work forms, and will therefore, in most cases, become more adapt to a changing environment.

One argument against the claim is that one needs the time spent in a university to reach a level of maturity required to work in the real world. Many students have not yet chosen a career before they enroll, and might need this time to realize one's potential within different fields. On the other side, there are those that are mature enough, and have known what they will do for years before they enroll.

An important aspect of the working world is how to present your opinions and such in an appropriate manner. This is dealt with in universities by the amount of papers, essays, and so on, that one has to write. These things also tend to have a deadline, which relates to another aspect of the working world; how to deal with stress.

One of the most important reasons for why one goes to university is the acquisition of knowledge. This is not only important to get a job, but also to be able to do said job in a satisfactory manner. There is, however, reason to argue that the knowledge one might obtain from the university, is not as specialized as what is required in the working world. The need for this broad field of knowledge is caused by the differences in various jobs one might apply for after one has finished one’s education.

One of the most accepted arguments against going to the university is the expenses. After one has finished one’s education, one might find oneself with a substantial amount of debt, which could cause problems when one enters the working world. Not only does this mean that one have to adjust the living conditions accordingly, but one might not be able to take certain jobs that are, among other things, too far away.

When one looks at all the pros and cons of the universities, in concord with what the working world entails, one could conclude with the importance of such an institution. There are, however, several aspects of this educational system that might not have been perfected, for the education of what one would consider an ideal employee.

Språk og Tenkning

Hvor mye har språk å si for hvordan vi tenker? Kan språket (eller språkene) vi bruker i det daglige liv forandre hvordan vi oppfatter ikke-verbale situasjoner?

Lingvisten Benjamin Lee Whorf sin lingvistiske relativitets hypotese sier at språk ikke bare har en innflytelse på tenkningen, men faktisk bestemmer hva man har mulighet til å tenke. Dette ville i såfall betydd at personer som kommer fra kulturer som bare har noen få ord for farger burde ha større vanskeligheter med å se hele fargespektrumet enn personer som har mange ord for dette.

Eleanor Rosch testet dette ved å studere Daniene fra New Guinea, siden de bare har to fargeord i deres språk. I motsetning til det den lingvistiske relativitetshypotese ville tilsi, så viste seg at de, i likhet med personer som har mange ord for farger, kunne skille mellom et vidt spekter av farger. En annen studie som involverte geometriske former og folk fra Mundurkú, som ikke har mange ord for dette, gav samme resultat.

En annen studie viser derimot, når en sammenligner barn fra Himba med engelske barn, vil de engelske være bedre til å skille mellom farger. Siden språket man snakker Himba har bare fem ord for basisfarger og engelsk har elleve, så vil dette støtte Whorf sin hypotese. Likevel så tror de fleste lingvister at språket ikke bestemmer hva man tenker, men heller bare har en innflytelse på tenkningen og hvor effektivt man kan kategorisere erfaringer.

Språk kan også påvirke våre oppfatninger, valg og konklusjoner. Studier viser, for eksempel, at det kan skape og opprettholde stereotyper. En studie der høyskoleelever skulle lese et av to utsagn, der et av dem brukte maskuline ord som "man" og "himself", mens det andre brukte mer kjønnsnøytrale ord som "people" og "themselves". Så skulle de si hvor attraktivt yrket som ble omtalt ville være for menn og kvinner. Dette resulterte i at de som leste utsagnet med maskuline ord svarte at yrket var mindre attraktivt for kvinner, enn det de som leste det andre utsagnet svarte.

I noen tilfeller kan språk, ikke bare ha innflytelse på hvordan vi tenker, men også hvor godt vi tenker. Et eksempel på dette er de matematiske ferdighetene, til personer fra en del asiatiske land i motsetning til personer fra mer vestlige land. En av grunnene til dette kan være ordene og symbolene de forskjellige språkene bruker for representere tall. Mange asiatiske språk gjør det mye enklere å lære de grunnleggende to-sifrede tallsystemene. På språk som Kinesisk og Japansk så vil elleve og tjue være ti-en og to-ti respektivt. Dette fører til at for eksempel et norskt barn vil ha problemer med å skjønne systemet i samme hastighet som et barn fra Kina eller Japan.

Det virker til at, selv om det kansje ikke bestemmer hvordan man tenker, så kan språk absolut påvirke hvordan man tenker i en del situasjoner. Hva slags språk man bruker i det daglige liv kan også ha fordeler og ulemper når det kommer til spesifikke felt.

Comparison

Even though both 'text 1' and 'text 2' are invitations, they differ on many points. The first thing that one sees might be the different ways of introducing the texts. 'Text 1' starts off with "we cordially invite you to attend (...)", which is a very formal introduction, although it uses 'we' and 'you' to make it more interactive. The second text on the other hand, uses, not only informal, but quite colloquial language. The introduction of this text is "You've got to be here for this!". The use of contracted forms and exclamation marks makes it clear from the start that one is dealing with a type of text that is intended for friends and the likes.

The first text is invitation to a workshop and gives informasion about this event. By looking at how this text is written, one can see that it is intended for 'experts' in the field. Names and abbreviations of the parties involved might not mean much to people with no involvement in this. i.e. "the ICT programme of the Sixth RTD Framework of the European Communities" and "e-Government policymakers". This cannot be said about 'Text 2', that can be understood by all.

The sentence length of the first text are very long and cumbersome. The whole text consists of 125 words, that make up only 4 sentences. That gives an average of 31 words in each sentence. A sentence should, according to the Plain English Campaign, consist of 15-20 words. The second text, however, has an average sentence length of 11 words per sentence. This is 20 words less than 'text 1', and a good example of how sentence length often depends on the formality of the text.

The words in the 'text 1' tends to be longer, Romanced words, which is commonly found in formal texts. The second text uses mostly smaller words with Germanic origin, as well as non-standard words like "Yo Ho Ho".

There is a notable difference in how the two texts presents the time the events will take place. The first text uses a formal approach with "Tuesday, March 11, 2008". 'Text 2' takes a different approach and uses a form closer to spoken English; "Friday 14th at 4pm".

The first text use the passives "(...) and addresses (...)" and "Improving access to (...)". The first is to focus on who this is directed to, and the second to make the statement stronger. 'Text 2' maintains the personal flavor and tends to use active sentence. The passive "Best outfit wins a prize", however, is used to focus attention on what it is that will win the price.

One might say that the differences betweens these two texts can be reduced to formality. This difference in formality can be contributed to the level required by the different events. The second text is an invitation to a birthday party, therefore a friendly tone is appropriate. The first text is an invitation to a more formal event, and requires a more serious tone.

One can find a great deal of difference between two texts, even though the texts might be just a single paragraph long. Advertisements, like 'Text 2', keep it simple because the goal is usually to be read by as many people as possible.
